About Me

I am a PhD student in Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering at the University of Pennsylvania. I am a member of the Patel Research Group, affiliated with the Penn Institute for Computational Science, since November 2019. I conduct research at the interface of biology, chemistry, and computational science.

Biology happens in water, therefore understanding how the smallest components of biological systems interact with water at the nanoscale is essential to glean insight into the complex chemistry of life. At the Patel group, I use high performance computing to study the effect of hydration on protein structure, stability, and interactions. My aim is to use the understanding we gain from these studies to help inform the design of future drugs and therapeutics.

My research methodology can be divided into three key steps:

  • The first step involves developing and applying enhanced atomistic simulation methods to observe and gather data about rare events several orders of magnitudes faster than traditional computational techniques. One technique that I use, which is central to the work we do at the Patel group, is INDUS.
  • The second step involves developing algorithms and frameworks to efficiently analyze the large volumes of data that these atomistic simulations yield. Two public frameworks which I'm currently writing for analysing my data are INDUSAnalysis and WHAM. Most of my code, however, is private at the moment.
  • The final, and most crucial step, involves developing new ways to visualize our results to help us communicate our insights to the scientific community.

I am passionate about using computation to solve challenging problems. Prior to starting my PhD at Penn, I worked in research settings at Caltech, JAIST (Japan), and IIT Gandhinagar (India) on a variety of interdisciplinary problems in applied physics, materials science, chemistry, and computer science. These included the study of interfacial fluctuations using molecular dynamics, heat transport in amorphous materials using molecular dynamics, performance benchmarking for density functional theory calculations, and theoretical computer science research in the field of computational social choice. I used computational tools and developed algorithms and frameworks - and in one instance, assembled parallel computing hardware - to solve these problems.

I was also a part-time competitive programmer, and participated in the ACM-ICPC India Regionals in 2017.

Software I've written for my research
INDUSAnalysis

Package for computing and analyzing various order parameters from solvated protein simulation data generated using the in-house implementation of dynamic INDUS in GROMACS.

Source Documentation
WHAM

Package for computing free energy profiles from multi-state MD data using the (binned) weighted histogram analysis method (WHAM) and the (binless) multistate Bennett acceptance ratio (MBAR) method.
